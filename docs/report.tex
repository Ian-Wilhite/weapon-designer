\begin{abstract}
We present a modular optimisation framework for the automated design of combat
robot spinning weapons.  The system is structured around five independently
configurable modules: Contact Profile, Cutout Generator, Manufacturability Pre-processing, Evaluation Objective, and Optimization Method. Profile parameterization methods are compared on the basis of Jacobian stability metrics and optimisation performance.
Three spline
profile families are compared against a frozen Fourier radial baseline: a
periodic cubic B-spline (C\textsuperscript{2}), a composite cubic
B\'{e}zier (C\textsuperscript{1}), and a centripetal Catmull-Rom spline
(C\textsuperscript{1}).  The enhanced objective replaces a formula-constant
bite-depth model with a kinematic Archimedean spiral simulation and replaces
a geometric structural proxy with coarse-mesh 2D plane-stress FEA evaluated
inside the optimisation loop.  Differential evolution drives a two-phase
search: Phase~1 optimises $N{=}12$ radial control points; Phase~2 optimises
polar superelliptic cutouts with analytical mass normalisation.
Experiments across three weapon archetypes (featherweight disk, compact bar,
and eggbeater) show that the enhanced objective reliably
detects structurally unsafe candidate designs that the baseline silently
accepts.  On the eggbeater case, the Catmull-Rom family achieves the best
score among enhanced variants and produces a weapon with 16\% higher MOI
than the Fourier baseline, demonstrating that the interpolation property of
Catmull-Rom aids exploration of naturally lobed geometries.  B-spline and
B\'{e}zier converge to competitive structural safety factors on the disk
archetype, with B\'{e}zier attaining safety factor~18.7 versus the baseline's
unexamined geometry.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Combat robot competitions demand spinning weapons that simultaneously maximise
stored kinetic energy, deliver large bite depth per tooth engagement, survive
centrifugal and impact stresses, and remain within strict mass and envelope
rules.  Manual design is iterative and heavily experience-dependent; even
expert builders rely on trial-and-error tuning of shape parameters whose
interactions are poorly understood.

Automated geometric optimisation has the potential to explore the design space
systematically and make engineering trade-offs explicit.  However, the utility
of any study depends critically on model fidelity.  A bite-depth formula that
is constant for a given RPM and weapon style cannot reward geometry that
genuinely increases engagement; a geometric structural proxy cannot detect
stress concentrations introduced by weight-relief pockets.  Existing
community practice relies on such simplified models or on manual topology
decisions, providing no principled way to evaluate the contribution of
individual design components.

This paper makes three contributions.  First, we describe a five-slot modular
framework in which every model component---profile family, interior
representation, bite model, structural solver, and normalisation
strategy---can be swapped independently without modifying any other module.
Second, we present an enhanced objective stack that corrects three known
baseline weaknesses: it replaces formula-constant bite with a kinematic
Archimedean spiral simulation, replaces a geometric structural proxy with
2D CST plane-stress FEA in the optimisation loop, and derives tooth count
from weapon geometry rather than hardcoded style parameters.  Third, we
report a controlled comparison of three spline profile families across four
weapon archetypes, using the frozen Fourier baseline as the research
reference.

\section{Profile Parametrisations}
\label{sec:profiles}

All profile families share the same interface:
\begin{equation}
  f(\boldsymbol{r},\, R_{\max},\, R_{\min},\, n_{\mathrm{eval}})
  \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Polygon} \mid \texttt{None}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{r} = [r_0, \ldots, r_{N-1}]$ is the vector of $N$ radial
control values, one at each angle $\theta_i = 2\pi i/N$.  The output is a
closed Shapely polygon with $n_{\mathrm{eval}} = 360$ vertices.
Figure~\ref{fig:splines} illustrates the same control-point set rendered by
each family.

\subsection{Piecewise-Linear (C\textsuperscript{0}, baseline for comparison)}
\label{sec:pwlinear}

The simplest possible closed profile: $N$ radial control values
$r_0,\ldots,r_{N-1}$ at equally-spaced angles, connected by straight chords.
Each parameter affects exactly two adjacent vertices and nothing else---the
maximally local parametrisation.  Its C\textsuperscript{0} boundary
introduces kinks at every control angle, making it unsuitable for
manufacturing but useful as a stability baseline.  Bounds are identical to the
spline families: $r_i \in [R_{\min},\,R_{\max}]$.

\subsection{Fourier Radial Series (optimiser baseline, frozen)}
\label{sec:fourier}

The baseline profile is a Fourier radial series:
\begin{equation}
  R(\theta) = R_{\mathrm{base}}
    + \sum_{k=1}^{K}\bigl(a_k \cos k\theta + b_k \sin k\theta\bigr)
  \label{eq:fourier}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\mathrm{base}}$ is the mean radius and the $2K$ coefficients
$(a_k, b_k)$ are the optimisation variables.  The Fourier series is
implemented in \texttt{parametric.py} and is frozen for the duration of this
study.  Its main limitation is \emph{global support}: perturbing a single
coefficient propagates oscillations around the full weapon boundary, and
abrupt parameter changes between DE steps can excite Gibbs-type ringing
when $K$ is moderate.

\subsection{Periodic Cubic B-Spline}
\label{sec:bspline}

Control point $i$ is placed at
$(r_i \cos\theta_i,\; r_i \sin\theta_i)$.
A periodic cubic B-spline is fitted through these $N$ Cartesian points
using \texttt{scipy.interpolate.splprep} with \texttt{per=True}, \texttt{k=3},
$s{=}0$.

\paragraph{Properties.}
C\textsuperscript{2} everywhere by construction.  The B-spline basis has local
support of width $4/N$ in parameter space, so moving control radius $r_i$
affects only the angular neighbourhood $[\theta_i \pm 4\pi/N]$.  No Gibbs
phenomenon occurs because spline degree is fixed at~3 regardless of~$N$.

\subsection{Composite Cubic B\'{e}zier}
\label{sec:bezier}

$N$ cubic B\'{e}zier segments are joined at the $N$ control points with
C\textsuperscript{1} continuity.  Tangents are computed via Catmull-Rom-style
central differences:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{T}_i = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\boldsymbol{P}_{i+1} - \boldsymbol{P}_{i-1}\right)
\end{equation}
with periodic index arithmetic.  The inner control points of segment $i$ are:
\begin{align}
  \boldsymbol{C}_{1,i} &= \boldsymbol{P}_i + \tfrac{1}{3}\boldsymbol{T}_i,\\
  \boldsymbol{C}_{2,i} &= \boldsymbol{P}_{i+1} - \tfrac{1}{3}\boldsymbol{T}_{i+1}.
\end{align}
Each segment is C\textsuperscript{1} but curvature may be discontinuous at
joins.  Support is marginally tighter than the B-spline: each control radius
affects at most two adjacent segments.

\subsection{Centripetal Catmull-Rom}
\label{sec:catmullrom}

The centripetal variant ($\alpha{=}0.5$) of the Catmull-Rom spline is evaluated
via the Barry--Goldman recursive algorithm~\cite{catmullrom1974}.  The knot
sequence is:
\begin{equation}
  t_0 = 0,\quad
  t_{k+1} = t_k + \|\boldsymbol{P}_{k+1} - \boldsymbol{P}_k\|^{0.5}.
\end{equation}
Unlike the B-spline and B\'{e}zier families, the Catmull-Rom spline
\emph{interpolates} every control point: the curve passes exactly through
$(r_i \cos\theta_i,\; r_i \sin\theta_i)$.  This makes $r_i$ the exact weapon
radius at angle $\theta_i$---a property that aids initialisation from
physically motivated seed shapes.  The centripetal parameterisation prevents
cusps and self-intersections that the uniform variant ($\alpha{=}0$) can
produce for widely-spaced control points.

\subsection{Stability Analysis: Jacobian Decomposition}
\label{sec:stability}

To quantify optimiser stability analytically we decompose the chain
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dO}{d\boldsymbol{x}}
  = \underbrace{\frac{\partial p}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}}_{J}^{\!\top}
    \underbrace{\frac{\partial O}{\partial p}}_{\boldsymbol{q}}
  \label{eq:chain}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{x}\in\mathbb{R}^N$ is the parameter vector,
$p(\boldsymbol{x})\in\mathbb{R}^A$ is the discretised polar profile
$r(\theta_i)$ sampled at $A{=}360$ angles, and $O$ is a fast geometric
objective (normalised MOI, mass utilisation, and COM balance; no FEA).
$J\in\mathbb{R}^{A\times N}$ is the profile Jacobian estimated by central
finite differences at the circular midpoint $\boldsymbol{x}_0$
(all $r_i=(R_{\max}+R_{\min})/2$) across eight step sizes spanning five
decades.  The profile sensitivity $\boldsymbol{q}$ is the minimum-norm
solution to $J^\top\boldsymbol{q}=\boldsymbol{g}$ (where $\boldsymbol{g}
=\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}}O$), obtained by \texttt{numpy.linalg.lstsq}.

\subsubsection{Singular Value Spectrum of $J$}

Figure~\ref{fig:stab_sv} shows the normalised singular value spectrum
$\sigma_k/\sigma_1$ of $J$ for all five parametrisations.  Bézier,
Catmull-Rom, and piecewise-linear each achieve full rank~12 with a gradual
decay; the ratio $\sigma_{12}/\sigma_1$ is approximately $0.013$, $0.010$,
and $0.011$ respectively.  Fourier radial loses two effective singular values
($\sigma_{11}\approx\sigma_{12}\approx 0$) because the high-frequency sine and
cosine modes have identical or near-identical influence on the polar profile at
$A{=}360$ samples.  B-spline is most severely affected: only 9 of 12 singular
values exceed the $1\%$ threshold, and the condition number is
$\kappa(J)\approx 6.8\times10^{12}$---the result of \texttt{scipy.splprep}'s
global Cartesian basis introducing near-cancelling combinations among
control-point directions.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/stab_sv.png}
  \caption{Normalised singular value spectra of the profile Jacobian
    $J=\partial p/\partial x$ at the circular midpoint ($N{=}12$).
    Bézier, Catmull-Rom, and piecewise-linear maintain full rank.

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Condition Number vs.\ Step Size}

Figure~\ref{fig:stab_cond} plots $\kappa(J)$ against the perturbation
magnitude $h$.  For all well-conditioned families the condition number is
stable across three decades of $h$, confirming that the Jacobian is a genuine
intrinsic property of the parametrisation rather than a numerical artefact.
B-spline's $\kappa$ declines slightly at large $h$ (approaching $h{=}25$\,mm)
because finite differences at such coarse scale blend several spline basis
functions and wash out the near-null modes---a fortuitous improvement that
does not persist for small $h$ values used during optimisation.

A smooth objective satisfies $\|\nabla_{\boldsymbol{x}}O(h)\|_2 \approx

mild decline at large $h$ ($>8$\,mm) reflects genuine nonlinearity over a
wide neighbourhood rather than non-smoothness.  B-spline shows a steeper fall
even at intermediate steps, consistent with its degenerate parameter

\subsubsection{Summary and Implications for Gradient-Based Optimisation}

The Jacobian analysis yields a clear recommendation: \textbf{Bézier and
Catmull-Rom are the most suitable parametrisations for gradient-based
refinement}.  Both achieve $\kappa(J)\approx76$--96, full rank, and perfect
gradient direction consistency.  Piecewise-linear matches these scores but
introduces C\textsuperscript{0} kinks that violate manufacturing constraints
and produce noisy contact-mechanics evaluations.  Fourier's intermediate
$\kappa\approx 1.1\times10^5$ and lost rank arise from high-frequency mode
aliasing; it would benefit from regularisation (e.g.\ a Tikhonov penalty on
high-$k$ coefficients).  B-spline's catastrophic $\kappa\sim10^{12}$
indicates that its three near-null parameter directions are effectively
\emph{invisible} to the optimiser: DE mutation steps that move along those
directions produce profiles indistinguishable from their parents, causing
stagnation and premature convergence consistent with the empirical
instability observed during baseline experiments.

\section{Evaluation Methods}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Baseline Objective}
\label{sec:baseline_obj}

The baseline objective (\texttt{objectives.py}) computes:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\mathrm{base}} = w_1 E + w_2 B + w_3 \mathit{IZ} + w_4 \mathit{Str} + w_5 \mathit{Sym}
\end{equation}
where $E$ is stored kinetic energy, $B$ is bite depth, $\mathit{IZ}$ is the
impact zone fraction, $\mathit{Str}$ is the structural score, and
$\mathit{Sym}$ is a symmetry penalty.

\paragraph{Formula bite.}
\begin{equation}
  b = \frac{v_{\mathrm{approach}}}{n_{\mathrm{teeth}} \cdot f_{\mathrm{rot}}}
\end{equation}
$n_{\mathrm{teeth}}$ is \emph{hardcoded} per weapon style (1, 2, or 3), so
the optimiser cannot increase the score by modifying tooth prominence.

\paragraph{Structural proxy.}
\texttt{structural.py} uses minimum wall thickness and section width at the
bore as a proxy for bending stiffness.  No FEA is performed.

\subsection{Enhanced Objective: Kinematic Spiral Bite}
\label{sec:spiral}

The enhanced objective (\texttt{objectives\_enhanced.py}) replaces both the
formula bite model and the structural proxy.

\paragraph{Physical model.}
In the weapon's rotating frame, the approaching opponent traces an
Archimedean spiral.  With angular velocity $\omega = 2\pi\,\mathrm{RPM}/60$
and closing speed $v$, the radial advance per radian is:
\begin{equation}
  v_{\mathrm{rad}} = \frac{v}{\omega} \quad [\text{mm/rad}].
\end{equation}
The opponent's position is:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\mathrm{enemy}}(\theta) = r_{\mathrm{start}} - v_{\mathrm{rad}} \cdot \theta.
  \label{eq:spiral}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Contact counting.}
The weapon's radial profile $r_{\mathrm{profile}}(\theta)$ is sampled at
720 equally-spaced angles.  A contact event occurs at any index $k$ satisfying:
\begin{align}
  r_{\mathrm{enemy}}(\theta_k)   &> r_{\mathrm{profile}}(\theta_k), \nonumber\\
  r_{\mathrm{enemy}}(\theta_{k+1}) &\leq r_{\mathrm{profile}}(\theta_{k+1}).
\end{align}

\paragraph{Effective bite depth.}
\begin{equation}
  b_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{v_{\mathrm{rad}} \cdot 2\pi}{n_{\mathrm{contacts}}}.
  \label{eq:bite}
\end{equation}
A smooth disk: $n_{\mathrm{contacts}} = 1$, maximum bite $\approx 15$\,mm
at 12\,000\,RPM and 3\,m/s closing speed.  Twelve small teeth:
$n_{\mathrm{contacts}} \approx 12$, bite $\approx 1.25$\,mm.
Figure~\ref{fig:spiral} shows Archimedean spiral overlays on an optimised
featherweight disk geometry with the contact points marked.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/spiral_contacts.png}
  \caption{Spiral contact visualisation for an optimised featherweight disk
    (B-spline profile, 12\,000\,RPM, $v{=}3$\,m/s).  Coloured curves are
    Archimedean spirals initiated from 20 angular offsets; orange dots mark
    contact events; red arrows are the tangential impact force directions
    applied as Neumann boundary conditions in the FEA.}
  \label{fig:spiral}
\end{figure}

\subsection{FEA-in-Loop Structural Scoring}
\label{sec:fea}

The enhanced stack replaces the geometric proxy with 2D constant-strain
triangle (CST) plane-stress FEA~\cite{logan2011fea} implemented in
\texttt{fea.py}.

\paragraph{Problem formulation.}
The weapon polygon is meshed at spacing \texttt{fea\_coarse\_spacing\_mm}
(10\,mm during optimisation; 4\,mm for final renders).  Centrifugal body force
per unit area:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{f}_c = \rho \omega^2 \boldsymbol{r}.
\end{equation}
All nodes inside the bore circle are fixed.  Optionally, tangential contact
forces derived from the spiral simulation are applied as Neumann boundary
conditions (Section~\ref{sec:spiral}) to capture impact-induced stress.

\paragraph{Stress metric.}
The safety factor is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathit{SF} = \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{yield}}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{VM,max}}}.
\end{equation}
High safety factors contribute positively to the objective; $\mathit{SF} < 1$
indicates structural failure and incurs a heavy score penalty, causing the
optimiser to reject the candidate.

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
At 10\,mm spacing a 75\,mm-radius disk produces approximately 200--400
elements, with each FEA solve taking 5--20\,ms on a single CPU core.  A
staged evaluation gate (\texttt{staged\_eval\_gate}) restricts full FEA to
candidates that pass cheaper geometry and kinematic checks first, reducing
total evaluation cost by approximately 5--20$\times$ depending on gate
selectivity.

\paragraph{FEA verification.}
Two analytical tests confirm that the CST implementation is physically correct.
Test~1 compares the FEA centre stress in a solid rotating disk against the
Lam\'{e} solution:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\mathrm{centre}} = \frac{\rho\,\omega^2 R^2 (3+\nu)}{8},
\end{equation}
obtaining 1.1\% error at $R{=}100$\,mm, 8\,000\,RPM.  Test~2 checks the
biaxial stress state at $r = 0.7R$ against the Lam\'{e} plane-stress formula:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\mathrm{VM}}(r) = \sqrt{\sigma_r^2 + \sigma_\theta^2 - \sigma_r\sigma_\theta},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_r(r) = \frac{\rho\omega^2(3{+}\nu)}{8}(R^2-r^2)$ and
$\sigma_\theta(r) = \frac{\rho\omega^2(3{+}\nu)}{8}\!\left[R^2 - \frac{1{+}3\nu}{3{+}\nu}r^2\right]$,
obtaining 0.7\% error against the analytical value of 14.5\,MPa.
Both tests pass with errors well below the 15\% threshold.
Figure~\ref{fig:fea_verify} shows the stress field and radial profile comparison.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fea_verification.png}
  \caption{FEA verification.  \emph{Left}: von-Mises stress field for a
    solid steel disk at 8\,000\,RPM (10\,mm mesh spacing); white dashed
    circles are analytical iso-stress contours.
    \emph{Centre}: per-element FEA stresses (blue dots) against the Lam\'{e}
    radial profile (red line); vertical markers indicate the two test
    locations.
    \emph{Right}: bar chart comparing analytical and FEA values; green
    annotations show the relative error (1.1\% and 0.7\% respectively).}
  \label{fig:fea_verify}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Physical Energy Transfer Objective}
\label{sec:physical_obj}

The enhanced objective (\texttt{objectives\_enhanced.py}) employs a weighted
sum whose weights remain exposed as tuning parameters.  To obtain a
\emph{single physical quantity} with no free weighting parameters, we introduce
the physical energy transfer objective in \texttt{objectives\_physical.py}.

\paragraph{Energy transfer model.}
The energy delivered to an opponent per contact event scales as the product
of stored kinetic energy, the squared cosine of the contact angle (normal
velocity component), and the bite fraction:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{transfer}} = \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \cdot \cos^2\!\theta_c \cdot
    \frac{b_{\mathrm{eff}}}{b_{\mathrm{max}}},
  \label{eq:etransfer}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_c$ is the contact angle returned by the spiral simulation,
$b_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the kinematic bite (Eq.~\ref{eq:bite}), and
$b_{\mathrm{max}} = v_{\mathrm{rad}} \cdot 2\pi$ is the single-contact
upper bound.  The objective is normalised against a theoretical reference:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{ref}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\, m_{\mathrm{budget}}\, R_{\max}^2\, \omega^2,
  \label{eq:eref}
\end{equation}
giving $E_{\mathrm{score}} = E_{\mathrm{transfer}} / E_{\mathrm{ref}} \in [0,1]$.

\paragraph{Hard constraints.}
Infeasible candidates return $E_{\mathrm{score}} = 0$ immediately without
soft penalty terms:
\begin{equation}
  \text{kill if:} \quad
  m > m_{\mathrm{budget}}, \;\;
  \mathit{SF} < 1.5, \;\;
  \Delta_{\mathrm{CoM}} > 0.03 R_{\max}.
\end{equation}
This eliminates the four free weighting parameters of the enhanced objective
while preserving all three physical constraints.  The physical mode is
activated via \texttt{evaluation\_mode = "physical"} in the configuration.

\section{Optimisation Framework}
\label{sec:optim}

\subsection{Differential Evolution}
\label{sec:de}

Both stacks use SciPy's \texttt{differential\_evolution}~\cite{storn1997de}
with strategy \texttt{best1bin}.  The enhanced optimizer
(\texttt{optimizer\_enhanced.py}) runs a two-phase search; the baseline runs
a single phase.

\subsection{Phase 1: Outer Profile}
\label{sec:phase1}

Phase~1 optimises $N_{\mathrm{bs}}$ control radii (default $N{=}12$) with
bounds:
\begin{equation}
  r_i \in [R_{\min},\; R_{\max}], \quad i = 0, \ldots, N-1.
\end{equation}
The Phase~1 objective is the full enhanced score with no cutouts.  This phase
uses 50\% of the total iteration budget.

Population seeding combines \emph{functional seeds}---lobed profiles with
$r(\theta) = R_0 + A_1\cos(n\theta) + A_2\cos(2n\theta)$---with Latin
hypercube samples, accelerating early convergence on naturally periodic
weapon geometries.

\subsection{Phase 2: Polar Superellipse Cutouts}
\label{sec:phase2}

Phase~2 fixes the best Phase~1 profile and optimises weight-relief pockets.
Each pocket is a superellipse in polar coordinates:
\begin{equation}
  \left|\frac{x'}{a}\right|^n + \left|\frac{y'}{b}\right|^n \leq 1,
\end{equation}
centred at polar position $(r, \phi)$ with semi-axes $a$, $b$ and exponent
$n$.  An analytical normalisation step rescales all semi-axes:
\begin{equation}
  s = \sqrt{\frac{A_{\mathrm{holes,target}}}{A_{\mathrm{holes,eff}}}}
\end{equation}
to enforce the mass budget to within 1\%.  During Phase~2 the mass constraint
is excluded from the penalty (mass is enforced analytically afterwards).
Phase~2 uses 25\% of the total iteration budget.

\subsection{Convergence Visualisation}
\label{sec:gif}

When \texttt{fea\_interval} $> 0$, a sidecar triplet is saved per step:
a two-panel FEA stress PNG, a compressed \texttt{.npz} of nodal data, and a
\texttt{\_meta.json} with step, phase, score, and full metrics.  These are
assembled post-hoc into \texttt{convergence\_phase*.gif} by
\texttt{fea\_replay.py}, enabling reanalysis at different colour scales
without re-running the optimiser.

\section{Surrogate-Assisted Search}
\label{sec:rom}

FEA is the dominant cost per objective evaluation (${\approx}300$\,ms at
10\,mm spacing).  To enable faster exploration of the parameter space, we
build a reduced-order model (ROM) that predicts the full von-Mises stress
field from design parameters, then use it as a surrogate inside the
optimisation loop.

\subsection{FEA Database}
\label{sec:fea_database}

A training database of $(N, M)$ stress fields is generated by evaluating
$N$ designs sampled quasi-randomly over the $d$-dimensional B-spline
parameter space $\Omega = [R_{\min}, R_{\max}]^d$ via the Sobol
sequence~\cite{sobol1967}.  Each design is:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Decoded from parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ to a
        Shapely polygon via \texttt{profile\_builder.build\_profile()}.
  \item Submitted to \texttt{fea\_stress\_analysis\_with\_mesh()} at coarse
        10\,mm spacing.
  \item Stress fields are interpolated from the per-design mesh onto a
        \emph{fixed reference mesh} (solid disk, 8\,mm spacing,
        $M{\approx}1200$ elements) using a \texttt{LinearNDInterpolator}
        with nearest-neighbour fallback for boundary extrapolation.
\end{enumerate}
The output is a matrix $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}$ of
reference-mesh von-Mises stresses and a parameter matrix
$\boldsymbol{\Theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$.

\subsection{Proper Orthogonal Decomposition}
\label{sec:pod}

The stress matrix is mean-centred:
$\widetilde{\mathbf{S}} = \mathbf{S} - \boldsymbol{\mu}$,
then decomposed via truncated SVD:
$\widetilde{\mathbf{S}} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top$.
The POD basis $\boldsymbol{\Phi} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times M}$ retains the
$k$ rows of $\mathbf{V}^\top$ that account for ${\geq}99\%$ of cumulative
variance:
\begin{equation}
  k = \min\left\{j \;\Big|\; \frac{\sum_{i=1}^j \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^K \sigma_i^2} \geq 0.99\right\}.
\end{equation}
Each training design is projected onto the basis to yield POD coefficients
$\boldsymbol{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times k}$, where
$\alpha_{n,i} = \widetilde{\mathbf{s}}_n \cdot \boldsymbol{\phi}_i$.

\subsection{Gaussian Process Surrogate}
\label{sec:gp}

One GP is fitted per POD mode: $\alpha_i(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \sim
\mathcal{GP}(0, k_{\mathrm{RBF}} + k_{\mathrm{White}})$, where inputs
are normalised to $[0,1]^d$.  Hyperparameters are optimised by maximising
the marginal log-likelihood with $n_{\mathrm{restarts}} = 3$.  Prediction:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{s}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) =
    \boldsymbol{\mu} + \hat{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,\boldsymbol{\Phi},
\end{equation}
with per-element uncertainty propagated as
$\operatorname{Var}[\hat{s}_j] = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma^2_i(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,\phi_{i,j}^2$.

\paragraph{Leave-one-out validation.}
On the 50-design pilot database ($d{=}8$, $k{=}37$), the LOO mean peak
stress error is \textbf{3.9\%}, well below the 10\% target.  On the full
$N{=}500$ database, $k{=}187$ modes are needed for 99\% variance (reflecting
the high geometric diversity of Sobol-sampled B-spline shapes), and GP
fitting takes approximately 54\,minutes.  Validation metrics on the full
database are reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}.  GP calibration
improves markedly as $N$ grows (calibration $R^2 = -5.9$ at $N{=}50$).

\subsection{UCB Active Learning}
\label{sec:ucb}

During surrogate-assisted optimisation, the acquisition function for each
candidate $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  a(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \hat{E}_{\mathrm{score}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \beta\,\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{\theta}),
  \label{eq:ucb}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{E}_{\mathrm{score}}$ is the surrogate-predicted physical score
and $\hat{\sigma}$ is the predictive standard deviation.  Full FEA is
triggered only when:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\hat{\sigma} > \sigma_{\mathrm{FEA,threshold}}$ (high uncertainty), or
  \item $a(\boldsymbol{\theta}) > 0.9 \cdot \hat{E}^*$ (near-optimal candidate), or
  \item every $N_{\mathrm{force}}$ evaluations (periodic refresh).
\end{itemize}
After each FEA call the new $(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{s})$ pair is added
to the training set and all $k$ GPs are updated.  The FEA call rate and
speed-up over plain DE are logged to \texttt{surrogate\_stats.json}.

\subsection{ROM Infrastructure Results}
\label{sec:rom_results}

\paragraph{FEA database.}
A database of $N{=}500$ designs was generated by Sobol sampling over the
8-dimensional B-spline parameter space in approximately 2.5~minutes
(${\approx}300$\,ms per design on a single core).  All designs satisfy the
physical constraints (SF~$\geq 1.5$, mass within budget), producing
$E_{\mathrm{score}} = 1.0$ for every sample because thin-ring-like shapes
concentrate mass near $R_{\max}$ and saturate $E_{\mathrm{transfer}} / E_{\mathrm{ref}}$.
This finding implies that the active-learning surrogate should be seeded with
constraint-boundary designs (low SF, mass near limit) to expose score variance.
The stress fields themselves exhibit substantial variation: the mean von-Mises
stress ranges from 6~MPa to 28~MPa across the database.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Enhanced Scores Differ from the Baseline}
\label{sec:discuss_scores}

The Fourier baseline consistently achieves higher raw \emph{scores} than the
enhanced variants.  This requires careful interpretation.  The baseline
objective does not evaluate structural safety; a design with
$\sigma_{\mathrm{VM}} \gg \sigma_{\mathrm{yield}}$ receives the same
structural contribution as a safe design.  By contrast, the enhanced objective
penalises SF~$< 1$ heavily.

This is most visible on the \emph{featherweight disk}: the B-spline optimizer
converges to a shape with SF~$= 0.3$ (yielded under centrifugal load at
12\,000\,RPM), while the B\'{e}zier and Catmull-Rom variants converge to
SF~$= 18.7$ and 26.9 respectively---designs that are structurally sound.  The
Fourier baseline would have accepted the B-spline's failed geometry and
rewarded it.  The enhanced FEA correctly rejects it, resulting in a lower
aggregate score but a physically valid design.

A similar pattern holds for the \emph{compact bar}: all three enhanced methods
find bar geometries with SF~$\leq 1.1$, confirming that the bar archetype is
structurally demanding at 8\,000\,RPM with the chosen material and thickness.
The baseline's 0.628 score for the compact bar is therefore misleading: it
optimises an unexamined geometry.

\subsection{Eggbeater: Catmull-Rom Outperforms the Baseline in Energy and MOI}
\label{sec:discuss_egg}

On the eggbeater case, Catmull-Rom achieves a stored energy of 16\,649\,J and
MOI of 21\,086\,kg\,mm$^2$---exceeding the Fourier baseline's 14\,269\,J and
18\,072\,kg\,mm$^2$ by 17\% and 17\% respectively.  This is the most striking
result in the study: the enhanced optimizer, despite its additional structural
penalty, discovers a \emph{better-performing} design on absolute physical
metrics.

We attribute this to the Catmull-Rom interpolation property.  The eggbeater
archetype has a naturally lobed outer profile ($n=3$~blades); because
$r_i$ is the exact weapon radius at $\theta_i$ for Catmull-Rom, the
functional-seed initialisation directly encodes the three-lobe structure in
the DE initial population.  The B-spline and B\'{e}zier families approximate
the same seeds through their basis, with some attenuation of the lobe
amplitude at the control-point scale.

\subsection{Profile Family Trade-offs}
\label{sec:discuss_profiles}

The three spline families represent a three-way trade-off:

\paragraph{B-spline (C\textsuperscript{2}) vs.\ B\'{e}zier (C\textsuperscript{1}).}
The B-spline provides the smoothest boundary, which is advantageous for stress
analysis: curvature discontinuities at C\textsuperscript{1} joins can
introduce stress concentrators in the FEA solution.  However, the B-spline's
four-segment support window is slightly wider than the B\'{e}zier's two-segment
window, meaning perturbations propagate slightly further.  In the featherweight
disk experiment, the B-spline optimizer converged to a lower-SF local optimum,
suggesting that its smoother landscape allows a wider initial exploration but
may also cross into structurally unsafe regions more readily.

\paragraph{Catmull-Rom interpolation.}
The interpolation property simplifies warm-starting from designer-specified
targets (set $r_i$ to the desired radius at angle $\theta_i$ exactly) and
makes bounds violations immediately apparent in a polar plot.  The cost is
the mild overhead of the Barry--Goldman recursive algorithm compared to a
\texttt{splev} call; both are negligible relative to FEA.

\subsection{Baseline Weaknesses Confirmed}
\label{sec:discuss_baseline}

The experimental results confirm all three intentionally documented baseline
weaknesses:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{bite\_mm()} returns a constant for a given style and RPM;
    the enhanced spiral simulation derives bite geometrically, enabling the
    optimizer to exploit tooth prominence.
  \item \texttt{\_num\_teeth()} is hardcoded per style; the enhanced tooth
    detection uses \texttt{scipy.signal.find\_peaks} on the sampled profile.
  \item \texttt{structural\_score()} is a geometric proxy with no material
    reference; the FEA safety factor detects actual yielding.
\end{enumerate}

The fact that the baseline produces \emph{higher scores} on three of the four
cases despite these weaknesses does not indicate it is a better optimiser: it
is optimising a different, less demanding objective.

\subsection{Mass Normalisation and Cutout Limitations}
\label{sec:discuss_cutout}

The analytical mass normalisation (Section~\ref{sec:phase2}) assumes uniform
pocket scaling preserves relative geometry, which holds only when all pockets
share the same aspect ratio.  For mixed layouts the scaling changes relative
proportions.  Furthermore, the DE population operates in the un-normalised
parameter space, which may cause clustering near the feasible mass boundary.

Free polar superellipse placement frequently yields physically unrealistic
pocket positions (e.g., pockets overlapping the bore or crossing each other).
The manufacturability conditioning module (\texttt{manufacturability.py})
enforces minimum concave fillet radii (2\,mm for waterjet cutting) as a
post-process, but does not address pocket placement heuristics.  Structured
interior templates (spoke, slot, web-offset) are implemented in
\texttt{interior\_templates.py} and represent a more physically motivated
Phase~2 representation; systematic comparison is deferred to future work.

\subsection{FEA Mesh Fidelity}
\label{sec:discuss_fea}

Coarse-mesh FEA (10\,mm spacing) is a deliberate approximation: safety factor
estimates are accurate to within 15--25\% of 4\,mm fine-mesh values for
typical shapes.  The approximation is systematically non-conservative in that
coarser meshes underestimate stress concentrations, so the optimizer may accept
designs whose fine-mesh SF is below the nominal threshold.  The fine-mesh
evaluation at the committed design stage mitigates this.  A formal
Richardson-extrapolation mesh-convergence study requires a committed set of
geometries and is left for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a modular parametric weapon design framework with five
independently configurable swap points, enabling rigorous component-wise
comparison.  Three spline profile families---periodic cubic B-spline,
composite cubic B\'{e}zier, and centripetal Catmull-Rom---were evaluated
against a frozen Fourier baseline across three weapon archetypes.

The primary finding is that the enhanced FEA-in-loop objective provides
structural accountability that the baseline lacks.  Designs that the baseline
accepts silently are correctly penalised when their centrifugal safety factor
falls below one.  On the eggbeater archetype, the Catmull-Rom family discovers
a design that exceeds the Fourier baseline in both stored energy (+17\%) and
moment of inertia (+17\%), demonstrating that the enhanced objective can yield
genuinely better designs when the profile family is well-matched to the weapon
archetype.

The Catmull-Rom interpolation property is identified as a key advantage for
lobed geometries: because the spline passes exactly through control points,
functional-seed initialisation directly encodes the desired periodicity, and
the optimizer begins in a structurally reasonable region of the design space.

Two additional contributions described in this paper complete the evaluation
pipeline.  The physical energy transfer objective (Section~\ref{sec:physical_obj})
eliminates all subjective weighting parameters: the single scalar $E_{\mathrm{score}}$
measures energy delivered per contact and is bounded analytically by $E_{\mathrm{ref}}$.
The surrogate-assisted search (Section~\ref{sec:rom}) builds a POD/GP reduced-order
model over the B-spline design space to reduce FEA call count; UCB acquisition
(Eq.~\ref{eq:ucb}) focuses expensive evaluations on high-uncertainty or near-optimal
regions.
